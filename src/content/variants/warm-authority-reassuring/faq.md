## FAQ

**What if the project doesn't work out?**
In my experience, projects don't fail suddenly — they fail slowly, when early warning signs go unaddressed. That's exactly what discovery is designed to catch. By the time we commit to a full build, I've assessed the risks, the scope, and the fit thoroughly enough to be confident in the outcome. I don't take on projects I don't believe I can deliver well, and I'd rather be straightforward about that early than let you discover it later.

**How involved do I need to be?**
You stay involved in the decisions that shape your business — priorities, direction, trade-offs — while I own the technical execution completely. I keep you informed in clear, direct language and make sure you always understand what's happening with your product. In my experience, the best outcomes happen when founders feel confident enough to focus on the business side, knowing the technical side is handled by someone who's done this before.

**How is this different from hiring an agency?**
At an agency, you're a client in a portfolio. Communication is mediated by account managers, work is distributed across teams, and no single person carries accountability for your product. I've worked alongside agencies enough to know where the model breaks down — the layers, the hand-offs, the diffusion of ownership. Here, it's one experienced person who is personally responsible for your entire build. That's a fundamentally different relationship.

**Can you handle a larger project?**
Yes. I've hired and managed teams of developers when the scope demanded it. The model scales, but the experience and accountability stay constant — I remain the single point of contact and the person making the technical decisions. I know when a project needs more hands and how to bring them in without disrupting what's already working.

**What happens when something goes wrong?**
I tell you — clearly, calmly, and with a plan. After a decade of building products, I can tell you that problems are not a sign of failure; they're a normal part of any complex build. What matters is having someone experienced enough to recognize them early and honest enough to surface them immediately. That's what you get.

**Who owns the code?**
You do. Everything I build — codebase, infrastructure, documentation — belongs to you completely. If we ever part ways, you'll have a well-organized product that any competent developer can pick up and continue. I build things to outlast the engagement, because that's what good work looks like.
