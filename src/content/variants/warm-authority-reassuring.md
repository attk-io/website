## How It Works

I've refined this process over a decade of working with founders. Each phase is designed to give you clarity and confidence at every step — no guessing, no hoping, no waiting in the dark.

### 1. Discovery

This is where I learn your vision and you learn how I think. You tell me about the product, the market, and what you're trying to accomplish. I ask the questions that surface the risks and decisions most founders don't see until later — the architecture choices that determine whether a product scales, the scope decisions that determine whether it ships on time. By the end, you'll have a clear, honest picture of what it takes to build what you're imagining. If I'm not the right fit, I'll tell you — and that assessment alone is worth the $1,500 session fee.

### 2. Plan

I take your vision and translate it into a technical strategy you can read, question, and own. Not a dense specification — a clear map of what gets built, in what order, and why. You'll understand every trade-off and every priority, in language that respects your intelligence without assuming technical expertise. This is also where I do something most builders don't: I actively cut scope. I've seen too many projects fail because nobody had the experience to say "not yet" to a feature that sounded important but would have derailed the timeline.

### 3. Build

Development happens in short, focused cycles. Each week you'll see real, working progress and receive updates written for a founder, not an engineer — what was done, what's next, and whether anything needs your attention. I've learned that founders don't want to manage the build. They want to understand it. That's what these updates are for: enough clarity to stay confident and make smart business decisions, without the burden of technical oversight.

### 4. Launch

The product goes live — not as a demo or a prototype, but as production-ready software in real users' hands. I handle everything required to make that happen: infrastructure, deployment, monitoring, security. These aren't glamorous, but they're the difference between a product that works on launch day and one that falls apart under real conditions. I've launched enough products to know exactly what this phase demands, and I don't cut corners on it.

### 5. Iterate

Once real users are interacting with your product, we have something better than assumptions — we have evidence. I'll walk you through what the data is telling us, what's technically possible for the next cycle, and what I'd recommend based on what I've seen work in similar products. You decide what matters most for the business. This is where the partnership deepens — and where having a technical partner with pattern recognition across many product builds becomes most valuable.

## FAQ

**What if the project doesn't work out?**
In my experience, projects don't fail suddenly — they fail slowly, when early warning signs go unaddressed. That's exactly what discovery is designed to catch. By the time we commit to a full build, I've assessed the risks, the scope, and the fit thoroughly enough to be confident in the outcome. I don't take on projects I don't believe I can deliver well, and I'd rather be straightforward about that early than let you discover it later.

**How involved do I need to be?**
You stay involved in the decisions that shape your business — priorities, direction, trade-offs — while I own the technical execution completely. I keep you informed in clear, direct language and make sure you always understand what's happening with your product. In my experience, the best outcomes happen when founders feel confident enough to focus on the business side, knowing the technical side is handled by someone who's done this before.

**How is this different from hiring an agency?**
At an agency, you're a client in a portfolio. Communication is mediated by account managers, work is distributed across teams, and no single person carries accountability for your product. I've worked alongside agencies enough to know where the model breaks down — the layers, the hand-offs, the diffusion of ownership. Here, it's one experienced person who is personally responsible for your entire build. That's a fundamentally different relationship.

**Can you handle a larger project?**
Yes. I've hired and managed teams of developers when the scope demanded it. The model scales, but the experience and accountability stay constant — I remain the single point of contact and the person making the technical decisions. I know when a project needs more hands and how to bring them in without disrupting what's already working.

**What happens when something goes wrong?**
I tell you — clearly, calmly, and with a plan. After a decade of building products, I can tell you that problems are not a sign of failure; they're a normal part of any complex build. What matters is having someone experienced enough to recognize them early and honest enough to surface them immediately. That's what you get.

**Who owns the code?**
You do. Everything I build — codebase, infrastructure, documentation — belongs to you completely. If we ever part ways, you'll have a well-organized product that any competent developer can pick up and continue. I build things to outlast the engagement, because that's what good work looks like.
