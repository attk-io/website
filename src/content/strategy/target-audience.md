## 1. Jobs To Be Done Analysis

### Core Job Story

> "When I've identified a market gap that requires custom software to capture, I want a technical partner who can take full ownership of turning my vision into a working product, so I can focus on the business while knowing the build is in capable hands."

### Functional Jobs

| Job                                     | Description                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                         |
| --------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Translate vision to architecture        | Turn a product concept into technical decisions, system design, and a buildable plan                                                                                                                                                                                |
| Build and ship custom software          | Design, develop, and deploy the core product — from first commit to production                                                                                                                                                                                      |
| Own the technical stack                 | Make sound technology choices without the founder needing to evaluate them                                                                                                                                                                                          |
| Manage scope                            | Ruthlessly prioritize what gets built and protect the project from scope creep                                                                                                                                                                                      |
| Handle DevOps and infrastructure        | Set up hosting, CI/CD, monitoring — the invisible work that keeps the product running                                                                                                                                                                               |
| Scale the technical team when needed    | Hire and manage additional developers as the project demands                                                                                                                                                                                                        |
| Be the company's technical spokesperson | Represent the company confidently in any context that requires technical credibility — investor meetings, partner conversations, due diligence, customer demos. The founder gets a polished, articulate technical presence without needing to hire a full-time CTO. |

### Emotional Jobs

| Job                                            | Description                                                                                                          |
| ---------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Feel confident the build is on track           | Know that timelines are being met and technical decisions are sound, without needing to verify everything themselves |
| Not feel overwhelmed by technical complexity   | Be able to engage with technical topics in plain language without feeling lost or dependent                          |
| Feel in control of their own company           | Maintain strategic ownership even while delegating the entire technical execution                                    |
| Feel like they chose the right person          | Avoid the regret and anxiety of a bad technical hire — especially given how high-stakes the decision is              |
| Not feel like they're being taken advantage of | Trust that scope, timelines, and costs are honest — no padding, no unnecessary complexity                            |

### Social Jobs

| Job                                        | Description                                                                                   |
| ------------------------------------------ | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Be taken seriously by customers            | Launch a product that looks and works professionally, not like an MVP held together with tape |
| Have a legitimate technical operation      | Be able to say "we have a strong technical team" — even if that team is one person            |
| Appear credible to investors and acquirers | Have a technically sound product that withstands scrutiny during fundraising or due diligence |

### Hiring Triggers

The moment a founder seeks someone like Christian is characterized by:

1. **A referral from a trusted contact** — In all observed cases, the founder didn't go searching. Someone they trusted said "you should talk to Christian." This means the trigger isn't a Google search or an ad — it's a conversation in the founder's existing network.

2. **A clear technical need they can't fill themselves** — The founder has enough clarity on their product concept to know they need custom software, but they lack the technical ability to build it or evaluate builders.

3. **A commitment to the venture** — These are not tire-kickers. They've quit jobs, invested personal capital, or spent months/years on groundwork (e.g., curating 150K rows of data). They're already in motion.

### Hiring Criteria

What founders evaluate (consciously or not) when deciding to work with someone like Christian:

| Criterion                                      | Why it matters                                                                                   |
| ---------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Trusted referral**                           | Reduces risk in a domain where the founder can't independently evaluate quality                  |
| **Ability to translate vision → architecture** | The founder needs to feel understood — that their idea has been heard and can be built           |
| **Willingness to take full ownership**         | They don't want to manage a developer; they want a technical partner who runs the technical side |
| **Clear communication in plain language**      | They need to stay informed and make business decisions without needing to learn to code          |
| **Track record of shipping**                   | Evidence that this person finishes things and gets them to market                                |

### Firing Criteria

What would cause a founder to lose confidence or walk away:

- Missed timelines without explanation
- Feeling out of the loop on technical decisions
- Scope growing without clear justification
- Sense that the builder is optimizing for technical elegance over business value
- Poor communication — going dark, jargon-heavy updates, defensive responses to questions

### Alternatives Considered

| Alternative                        | Why founders might consider it         | Why it falls short                                                                                                 |
| ---------------------------------- | -------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Dev agency**                     | Appears scalable, professional, "safe" | Founder becomes a ticket in a queue; no single owner; communication layers add friction and cost                   |
| **Freelance developers**           | Cheaper hourly rate, flexible          | Founder must manage them — becomes a part-time CTO role they're not equipped for; quality varies wildly            |
| **Finding a technical co-founder** | Equity-based, fully aligned            | Extremely hard to find; requires giving up significant equity; co-founder relationship is a marriage — high stakes |
| **No-code / low-code tools**       | Fast, cheap, no technical dependency   | Hits a ceiling quickly; doesn't differentiate; hard to maintain or scale; investor/acquirer scrutiny               |
| **Doing nothing / waiting**        | No risk, no cost                       | Market window closes; competitors move; founder momentum dies                                                      |

## 2. Value Proposition Canvas

### Customer Profile

#### Customer Jobs

_(Consolidated from JTBD above)_

1. Get a custom software product built and launched
2. Make sound technical decisions without technical expertise
3. Stay focused on the business (sales, fundraising, customers) while the product gets built
4. Manage risk — avoid wasting money on a build that doesn't ship or doesn't work
5. Build something that can withstand external scrutiny (investors, acquirers, customers)

### Fit Analysis

**Strong fit:**

- The core functional job (build and ship software) maps directly to ATTK's primary service
- The emotional jobs (confidence, control, not being overwhelmed) are addressed by the communication style and scope management approach
- The biggest pains (can't evaluate quality, fear of bad hire, communication gap) are mitigated by the referral model and Christian's direct involvement
- The "technical co-founder substitute" positioning is a strong differentiator vs. agencies and freelancers

**Potential gaps to address in messaging:**

- **Bus factor** — The single-person model is a strength (no hand-offs, direct accountability) but could also be perceived as a risk. This is a legitimate gap with no operational strategy in place yet — a separate workstream to address later. For now, messaging should lean into the strengths of the model without overclaiming on continuity.
- **Scalability perception** — A solo practitioner may not seem equipped for larger builds. The fact that Christian has hired and managed teams of 3+ developers addresses this but isn't immediately obvious.
- **Price anchoring** — Founders comparing against freelancer rates or no-code tools may experience sticker shock. The value needs to be framed against the _cost of the alternative_ (failed builds, wasted months, lost equity to a co-founder).

## 3. Synthesized Target Audience Profile

### Who They Are

A bootstrapped solo founder — typically non-technical — who has identified a market gap that requires custom software to capture. They may be a first-time founder or a serial entrepreneur, but in either case they have:

- A clear product vision (not just a vague idea)
- Skin in the game (quit a job, invested personal capital, spent significant time on groundwork)
- Enough business acumen to know what they need built, even if they can't build it themselves
- A network that includes someone who knows Christian (the referral channel)

They are **not** looking for a quick buck, not building an ad-supported business, and not looking for the cheapest option. They value quality, speed, and trust over price.

### What They're Going Through

They're at a specific inflection point: the concept is clear, the market opportunity is real, and the next step is _building the thing_. This is simultaneously exciting and terrifying because:

- The software is the business — if the build fails, the venture fails
- They can't evaluate technical quality themselves, so choosing a builder feels like a leap of faith
- They're burning time and money every day they don't have a product in market
- They've heard horror stories about failed development projects, blown budgets, and missed deadlines

A trusted contact has pointed them toward Christian. They're not shopping around on Upwork — they're following a warm referral because they know they need someone good and they can't independently verify "good."

### What They Need to Hear

Messages that would resonate, given their jobs, pains, and gains:

1. **"I take ownership"** — They need to know that the technical side is handled. Not managed. Not coordinated. _Handled._ By one person who is accountable.

2. **"I've done this before, and the products are still running"** — Proof that this isn't theoretical. Real products, real outcomes, real longevity.

3. **"I'll protect your scope"** — This is unexpected value. Founders don't know to ask for this, but it's one of the things past clients valued most.

4. **"You'll understand what's happening"** — They won't be left in the dark or buried in jargon. They'll become fluent in their own product's technical landscape.

5. **"Your product will be built to last — and to sell"** — For founders with exit ambitions, knowing the product can withstand due diligence is a major gain. For all founders, knowing the product is built on a solid foundation provides confidence.

6. **"We move fast"** — Speed matters. Every month without a product is a month of opportunity cost.

### What Would Turn Them Off

Messaging anti-patterns to avoid:

| Anti-pattern                                       | Why it fails                                                                           |
| -------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **"We build apps"** — generic agency language      | Sounds like a commodity service; doesn't convey ownership or partnership               |
| **Jargon-heavy technical language**                | Alienates the non-technical founder; makes them feel like an outsider                  |
| **Emphasizing team size or process**               | They don't want a big team or a formal process — they want one person who gets it done |
| **"Move fast and break things"**                   | Implies carelessness; these founders are risking real money and time                   |
| **Price-first messaging**                          | Attracts cost-shoppers, not value-seekers; misaligns the audience                      |
| **Overpromising speed**                            | "Launch in 2 weeks" sounds like a scam to anyone with a real product idea              |
| **Startup bro culture**                            | Buzzwords like "disrupt," "10x," "crushing it" signal immaturity, not reliability      |
| **Stock photos of diverse teams in glass offices** | Reads as corporate and impersonal; contradicts the solo practitioner model             |
